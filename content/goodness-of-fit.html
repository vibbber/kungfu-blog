"Difficult" Isn't a Trait

Why the same child thrives in one setting and struggles in another

Roy was a "good baby."

When he cried, his parents could redirect his attention in seconds. Upset about a wet diaper? Show him a toy. Frustrated during a car ride? Point out the window. He was easily soothed, endlessly distractible, a joy to be around.

His parents congratulated themselves on their easy child.

By the time Roy was eight, his mother used different words: "impossible," "doesn't listen," "can't be counted on." She nagged constantly. He tuned her out constantly. They were locked in a cycle neither could escape.

What Changed?

Not Roy. Roy's distractibility — the exact same trait — stayed constant. What changed was what the world expected of him.

As a baby, distractibility meant "easy to soothe." As a child, distractibility meant "can't focus, forgets everything, never listens."

The trait was identical. The label flipped from gift to curse.

What Is "Goodness of Fit"?

Psychologists call it goodness of fit: the match (or mismatch) between a child's temperament and the environment's demands.

"Difficult" doesn't describe the child. It describes the gap between who they are and what the environment expects.

An active child in an athletic family? Good fit. That same child in a family that values quiet, controlled behavior? Poor fit. The child feels wrong. The parents feel frustrated. Everyone suffers.

Same child. Same trait. Different fit.

Why Do We Call Some Children "Difficult"?

Here's what the research actually shows about traits we call "difficult":

Surprising Findings

Fear/withdrawal (labeled "difficult") actually predicts higher conscience and lower aggression later in life.

A long attention span is "easy" when they amuse themselves, "difficult" when you call them for dinner.

Negative emotionality is "difficult" in Western cultures but seen completely differently in others.

When you label a child "difficult," you're describing the fit, not the child.

How Do You Know If It's a Fit Problem?

When your child is struggling in a situation, run through these four questions:

Four Diagnostic Questions

Ask yourself these when your child is struggling:

1. What Trait?

What temperament trait is this situation pressing on? (Fear, high activity, distractibility, low adaptability?)

2. What Demands?

What does this environment demand that clashes with my child's temperament?

3. Same Trait, Different Age?

Was this trait seen positively before? Has the situation changed, not the child?

4. Good Fit Where?

Where does this trait serve my child well? What environments bring out its strengths?

How Can the Same Trait Be Both "Good" and "Bad"?

Every trait has a situation where it's an asset and a situation where it's a liability. The difference is the fit, not the trait.

Trait "Difficult" Framing Fit-Aware Framing
High activity "Can't sit still, always in trouble" "Needs physical outlets; environment asks for too much stillness"
Fearful / shy "Too clingy, won't try things" "Cautious and careful; needs gradual introductions and time to warm up"
High intensity "So dramatic, overreacts to everything" "Experiences emotions strongly; needs more support during transitions"
Distractible "Never listens, forgetful" "Notices everything; needs fewer competing stimuli and shorter task segments"
Low adaptability "Rigid, inflexible, fights every change" "Needs predictability; give advance warning before transitions"

What Could Roy's Parents Have Done Differently?

If they'd understood goodness of fit, here's how they could have changed their approach:

Four Steps to Improve the Fit

1

See the trait, not the behavior

"Roy doesn't listen" becomes "Roy is distractible." It's not defiance — it's temperament. "He won't come to dinner" is about attention persistence, not disrespect.

2

Adjust the press

Reduce the environmental demand on that trait. For Roy: fewer competing stimuli, shorter instructions, visual reminders instead of verbal nagging.

3

Find the strength side

Distractibility = noticing everything. Where is that an asset? Creative thinking? Spotting things others miss? Find those situations and create more of them.

4

Communicate the fit

Tell teachers and caregivers what works, not what's "wrong." Say "Roy does best with one instruction at a time" — not "Roy has attention problems."

The Bottom Line

A child labeled "difficult" is almost always a child in a poor-fit environment.
Your job isn't to fix the child. It's to improve the fit.
